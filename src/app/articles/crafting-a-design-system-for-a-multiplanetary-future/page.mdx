AI is all the rage. The tech giants of the world are locked in an all-out sprint toward AGI, each breakthrough celebrated like a moon landing, each release pushing the boundary of what machines can do. But amid the noise and momentum, it’s hard to ignore a familiar warning. As Dr. Ian Malcolm put it, “Your scientists were so preoccupied with whether or not they could, they didn’t stop to think if they should."

<Image src={futureOfAi} alt="" />

And that’s the tension defining this moment: not just a race for capability, but a reckoning with consequence. Because the question is no longer if we can build something powerful—it’s whether we’re prepared to live with what comes next.

For most of human history, inefficiency wasn’t a flaw—it was the point. We meandered, we hesitated, we made mistakes, we learned slowly. The friction of being human—forgetfulness, emotion, bias, curiosity—wasn’t just tolerated; it was the engine of creativity and meaning.

Now we are building systems designed to eliminate all of that.

Large language models don’t get tired. They don’t second-guess. They don’t need time to “think” in the way we do. They optimize, compress, and produce at speeds that make human cognition feel quaint by comparison. In a world increasingly optimized for output, where does that leave the inefficient human?

The economist John Maynard Keynes once predicted that technological progress would lead to a 15-hour workweek, freeing humanity for “the art of life itself.” Instead, we’ve often filled efficiency gains with more work, more expectations, more pressure. AI threatens to accelerate that paradox. If machines can do in seconds what once took us hours, the bar doesn’t lower—it rises.

Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI, has said, “AI will probably most likely lead to the end of the world, but in the meantime, there’ll be great companies.” It’s a half-joking line, but it captures something real: the incentives driving this wave are not philosophical—they’re economic. Speed, scale, and dominance.

And humans, by comparison, are slow.

But slowness has value.

The philosopher Hannah Arendt warned about a world where efficiency overtakes judgment, where thinking itself becomes secondary to process. She described the danger of “thoughtlessness”—not stupidity, but the absence of reflection. In many ways, hyper-efficient AI systems risk institutionalizing that very condition: answers without understanding, decisions without deliberation.

Humans, inefficient as we are, pause. We doubt. We change our minds. We ask “why” long after “how” has been solved.

That matters.

Because intelligence is not just about producing correct outputs. It’s about context, meaning, and values. A model can generate a flawless essay in seconds, but it does not care whether it is true. It does not wrestle with uncertainty. It does not feel the weight of being wrong.

We do.

And that weight—the friction of caring—is where ethics lives.

There’s also a deeper question lurking beneath the surface: what are we optimizing for?

If the goal is pure efficiency, then yes, humans are a bottleneck. We’re inconsistent, emotional, expensive. We need sleep. We burn out. We argue. We take detours. From a purely computational perspective, we are wildly inefficient systems.

But if the goal is meaning, the equation changes.

Jaron Lanier, a pioneer of virtual reality and a longtime critic of Silicon Valley’s excesses, argues that technology should “honor human dignity.” Not replace it, not abstract it away, not reduce it to data points—but amplify it.

The danger is not that AI becomes more capable than us. It’s that we begin to measure ourselves by its standards.

When speed becomes the metric, reflection looks like weakness. When output is king, contemplation looks like waste. When optimization dominates, humanity starts to look like a bug instead of a feature.

And yet, history suggests something else.

Every major technological shift has forced a redefinition of human value. The industrial revolution replaced physical labor with machines. The information age automated calculation and storage. Each time, humans moved—not out of relevance, but into new domains of meaning.

The question isn’t whether AI will outperform us in specific tasks. It already does. The question is whether we will allow those tasks to define our worth.

Because the things that remain hardest to automate are also the things we tend to undervalue: empathy, taste, judgment, moral reasoning, the ability to sit with ambiguity.

The “inefficient” parts.

So where does the inefficient human fit in?

Not at the margins, but at the center—if we choose it.

We are the ones who decide what problems are worth solving. We are the ones who define success. We are the ones who bear the consequences. AI can generate possibilities, but it cannot assign meaning to them.

That’s still our job.

The future isn’t a binary where humans either keep up or fall behind. It’s a negotiation. A design choice. A set of values encoded not just in our systems, but in how we choose to live alongside them.

We can build a world that optimizes away everything that makes us human.

Or we can build one that makes space for it.

And maybe that’s the real question—the one we should have been asking all along:

Not where the inefficient human fits in…

…but whether we’re willing to admit that inefficiency is the point.
